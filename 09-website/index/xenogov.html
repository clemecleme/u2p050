The inaugural project of our speculative start-up aims to tokenize and auction the United States
of America through a blockchain-implemented constitution. We’ve selected the American constitution as
our prototype for several compelling reasons. Firstly, its concise and straightforward nature makes it ideal
for blockchain implementation, especially from an originalist perspective that focuses on the fundamental
relationships between branches of government. Secondly, the profound emotional and cultural investment
Americans have in their constitution provides an excellent foundation for a provocative public auction
and communication strategy. This is evidenced by events like constitutionDAO, which raised over $60
million in cryptocurrency to bid on an original copy of the Constitution, demonstrating the potential
for significant public engagement with our project. Lastly, as the oldest democratic constitution crafted
by and for the people, the U.S. Constitution represents a perfect subject for our artistic exploration. Our
project simultaneously pays homage to this democratic legacy while interrogating the commodification
of governance in the digital age. By tokenizing the implementation of this foundational document, we
aim to spark dialogue about the intersection of historical democratic principles, blockchain technology,
and the future of governance, all within the framework of contemporary conceptual art.
The implementation of a blockchain-based constitution using Solidity leverages an advanced
architecture combining the Diamond pattern and nested proxy contracts, with additional facets
representing the various branches of government. At its core, the “DiamondConstitution” contract acts
as the central hub, utilizing the Diamond pattern for flexibility and upgradeability. This main contract
delegates calls to multiple facets, each representing different constitutional elements or governmental
branches. For instance, we create separate facets for the Executive, Legislative, and Judicial branches. The
Legislative facet could further be divided into sub-facets for the House of Representatives and the Senate,
each with its own internal organization and voting mechanisms.
u2p050 proposes to create a speculative start-up, named XenoGov, that aims to sell digital states
to existing States by implementing Constitutions as smart contracts on the blockchain. Recognizing that
current political climates may not be ready for blockchain-based governance, the company positions
itself as a conceptual art project that is fully functional but not yet commercially viable. This approach
allows the start-up to exist in a liminal space between art and technology, pushing the boundaries of
what a start-up can be by auctioning conceptual projects and creating a speculative market for ideas that
society isn’t prepared to accept as practical products. Conceptually, it asserts that any political regime
can be implemented as computable functions on a blockchain, allowing for multiple, even ideologically
opposed, state systems to coexist on the same platform. The long-term strategy employs the concept of
the Overton window—the range of ideas acceptable in public discourse—to gradually shift perceptions.
By initially operating as an art-focused enterprise, the company lays groundwork for potential future
transformation into a real-world provider of blockchain-based governance solutions, should political
attitudes evolve to embrace such technologies.

Our key assertion is that any administrative system defined in a constitution can be implemented
as a set of computable functions on a Turing machine, and by extension, on a Turing-complete blockchain.
This principle applies universally, regardless of the political ideology or structural complexity of the system
in question. The profound implication is that a single Turing-complete blockchain can simultaneously
host multiple, even ideologically opposed, state systems. For instance, it could run both the People's
Republic of China's Constitution and the United States Constitution concurrently, each operating as an
independent set of smart contracts within the same blockchain environment. This capability stems from
the blockchain's nature as a universal computational substrate, capable of executing any computable
function. By encoding constitutional provisions, administrative procedures, and governance mechanisms
as smart contracts, we create digital parallels of entire state systems. This simultaneous implementation
of diverse governance models on a unified blockchain platform not only demonstrates the universal
computability of administrative systems but also opens up unprecedented possibilities for comparative
governance studies, political experimentation, and the creation of parallel, non-geographically bound
state entities in the digital realm.
We want to situate this project within the rich tradition of legal art and institutional critique,
drawing significant parallels to seminal works in art history. Like Yves Klein's "Zones of Immaterial
Pictorial Sensibility" and Tino Sehgal's oral art transactions, it challenges conventional notions of
ownership, value, and documentation in art. By implementing constitutional systems on a blockchain, it
echoes the conceptual approach of artists like Hans Haacke, whose "MoMA Poll" questioned institutional
power structures. The project's focus on smart contracts as a medium for governance resonates with the
evolving concept of "contract art", exemplified by the Projansky contract and its adaptations by artists
like Cady Noland. Moreover, in its exploration of administrative systems as computable functions, the
work engages with themes of authorship and originality similar to Kenneth Goldsmith's appropriation
art, while pushing the boundaries of what constitutes a legal artwork. By merging blockchain technology
with constitutional law, this project not only continues the legacy of institutional critique but also expands
the definition of legal art into the digital realm, creating a new form of conceptual artwork that exists at
the intersection of law, technology, and governance.
The Executive facet might include functions for presidential powers and cabinet operations,
while the Judicial facet could handle case management and judicial review processes. Amendments are
implemented through a system of nested proxy contracts, allowing for a hierarchical structure of laws
where newer amendments can modify or override older ones. This approach mirrors the real-world
constitutional amendment process. Access controls ensure that only authorized entities can propose
and ratify amendments or execute branch-specific functions. For example, only addresses registered as
Representatives could participate in House votes within the Legislative facet. By combining the Diamond
pattern, branch-specific facets, and nested proxies, we create a highly flexible, upgradeable, and realistic
system. This structure accurately represents the complex, layered nature of a constitutional government,
complete with checks and balances, while harnessing the immutability and transparency of blockchain
technology.
